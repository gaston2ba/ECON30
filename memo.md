# Memo

## Working project frame

### Provisional title
Urban Design and Third Places: Public Space, Modernization, and Exclusion in Paris and Mexico City

### Focused research question
How did elite-led urban redesign in Paris and Mexico City transform access to public third places, and who gained or lost from those changes as both cities presented themselves as modern capitals?

### Working hypothesis
In both cities, redesign projects expanded formal public space and encouraged new forms of leisure, circulation, and commerce. At the same time, those spaces were not neutral: they helped stage order and modernity, favored elites and central districts, and often pushed poorer residents or informal uses to the margins.

## Narrowed comparison frame

- Primary comparison: public parks, promenades, and boulevards as visible spaces of sociability.
- Main outcome to track: access and exclusion in public life.
- Secondary outcomes: commercial activity, prestige, surveillance, and state control.
- Paris time window: roughly 1853-1870, centered on Haussmann and the Second Empire.
- Mexico City time window: roughly 1876-1911, centered on the Porfiriato and late nineteenth-century modernization.

## Provisional case-study sites

### Paris
- `Jardin des Tuileries`: useful for thinking about the transition from elite-controlled garden space to public urban leisure.
- `Bois de Boulogne` or `Parc des Buttes-Chaumont`: examples of Second Empire park-making tied to prestige, circulation, and new urban recreation.
- `Boulevard Haussmann` / the Grands Boulevards: good for tracking circulation, display, commerce, and cafe life.

### Mexico City
- `Chapultepec Park`: strong parallel to Parisian elite-to-public green space narratives.
- `Alameda Central`: a central public promenade with a long history that can show shifting access and urban symbolism.
- `Paseo de la Reforma`: the clearest Porfirian comparison to boulevard-style modernization and monumental urban display.

## Comparison sheet

| Category | Paris | Mexico City |
|---|---|---|
| Planning goals | Circulation, sanitation, imperial prestige, political order | Order and progress, elite modernization, sanitation, international image |
| Main political context | Second Empire / Haussmannization | Porfiriato / centralizing modernization |
| Key spaces | Parks, boulevards, promenades, cafe districts | Parks, promenades, boulevards, monumental avenues |
| Likely beneficiaries | Property owners, commercial elites, middle and upper classes | Political and economic elites, central districts, investors, middle and upper classes |
| Likely costs | Displacement, class sorting, tighter social control | Uneven investment, exclusion, prestige-centered development, possible displacement |
| Core evidence | Urban plans, maps, visual materials, secondary history, commercial geography | Urban plans, maps, photographs, modernization history, green-space and inequality scholarship |

## Notes on evidence to collect

- Historical maps showing the spatial layout of parks, boulevards, and adjacent neighborhoods.
- Images or postcards that show how these spaces were presented and used.
- Readings that help distinguish between public access in theory and real access in practice.
- Any evidence on who funded, regulated, or policed these spaces.
