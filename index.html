Two capitals, two ways of building a city.

In the 1850s, Baron Haussmann redrew Paris from above — twelve boulevards driven through working-class neighborhoods by imperial decree, a third of the medieval city demolished, an estimated 325,000 residents pushed to the periphery. Half a century later, Porfirio Díaz tried to do the same in Mexico City, importing the Haussmann template directly. He partly succeeded. But after WWII, rural migrants arrived faster than any government could plan for, and they built the city themselves: houses first, streets later, sewers later still. Today roughly 40% of Mexico City lives in housing that began informally.

These are not arbitrary cases. Paris and Mexico City are the largest cities of two former imperial systems — French and Spanish — and both were reshaped in the 19th century by elites consciously importing European planning ideals. They are roughly comparable in scale, both governed today by progressive municipalities, both wrestling with gentrificationWealthier residents and investment moving into a working-class neighborhood, often improving amenities while raising rents and displacing long-term residents. and the politics of who their public space is for. They differ, sharply, in one variable: the capacity and willingness of the state to plan. That makes them a useful pair.

Neither model is the hero of this story. Haussmann's Paris is also a story of displacement and class engineering. The colonias populares of Mexico City are also a remarkable feat of collective self-organization — a city built by the people who had to live in it, against the indifference of the state that should have housed them. We are not asking which city did it right. We are asking what kind of inequalityHere: the unequal distribution of income, housing, transit, and public space across neighborhoods in the same city — inequality you can see on a map. each style of city-building produced, and whether the quality of urban planningThe deliberate organization of land, infrastructure, and public space — from zoning and transit to parks and housing., measured at the neighborhood level, is associated with who is rich and who is poor — not whether planning alone causes wealth (see Findings).

This project compares the 20 arrondissements of Paris with the 16 alcaldías of Mexico City — units of roughly the same scale, in cities that are, in almost every other respect, opposites. Deeper histories unfold in the Paris and Mexico City timelines below.

The result, we hope, is a small contribution to a much older conversation: about who gets to draw the lines, who has to live inside them, and what spills across the boundary in either direction.

III · Our Index

An index of urban planning quality.

To compare neighborhoods across two very different cities, we built an index of urban planning quality — a score from 0 to 100 that combines six measurable features of how well a neighborhood is built and served. Each component is, as best we can manage, upstream of income.

Use the checkmarks to include or exclude pillars from the composite score. Each pillar keeps its narrative sealed behind a Why? tab so the grid stays legible until you need the fine print.

For Mexico City, component scores are drawn from our INEGI-based workbook (data/mexico/workbook/DataMexCity.xlsx, 1860–2020 panel): green space per capita (SEDEMA), transit station-equivalents per 100k residents, population density, museums/libraries per 100k, and housing infrastructure (% electricity, piped water, drainage, hospital access). Each is min–max normalized to 0–100 across the 16 alcaldías for 2020. For Paris, scores use INSEE population (2022), Paris Open Data green space and libraries, OSM metro stations, Airparif PM₂.₅ (citywide), and Filosofi 2021 median income — see data/paris/.

The Index · Build Your Own 6 of 6 active
Park access
Measurement

CDMX: green space m² per resident (SEDEMA, 2020). Paris: m² of public green space per resident (Paris Open Data, 2022 snapshot).

Rationale

Tree canopy and soft ground are expensive to retrofit; park proximity is a durable public investment that usually predates the wave of gentrificationNeighborhood ascent in rents and occupancy costs after amenities improve, sometimes displacing long-term renters. rents, making it useful as an upstream planning input.

Transit commute
Measurement

CDMX: transit station-equivalents per 100k residents (1860–2020 panel). Paris: cumulative metro stations per 100k residents (OSM/Overpass, 2020 decade).

Rationale

Network speed shapes labor‑market access; we avoid counting private vehicles because they track income more tightly than trains or BRT.

Productive density
Measurement

CDMX & Paris: residents per km² (INEGI / INSEE), min–max normalized within each city so extreme sprawl and extreme emptiness both score lower.

Rationale

Urban economics treats density as throughput for agglomeration, but unmanaged crowding destroys service quality — we penalize tails.

Third spaces
Measurement

CDMX: museums + libraries per 100k (2020). Paris: libraries (Paris Open Data) + museums (Wikipedia list) per 100k (2022).

Rationale

These are legislated public third places, closer to planning decisions than cafés (which we treat as outcomes downstream of income).

Walkability
Measurement

Composite of park access + third-space scores (average of the two normalized pillars) — a walkability proxy until street-network data is added.

Rationale

Walking fabric is politically cheap to advertise but unevenly enforced; our metric rewards continuous mesh, not billboard claims.

Basic infrastructure
Measurement

CDMX: average of % electricity, piped water, drainage, and hospital access (2020). Paris: inverted citywide PM₂.₅ background (Airparif, same value all arrondissements in this draft).

Rationale

These are the least sexy line items in a budget but the first ones withheld from informalSettlements built ahead of formal cadastral recognition; services often arrive late or through political negotiation. settlements — so they signal state capacity.

Index = ( ⅙ · P + ⅙ · T + ⅙ · D + ⅙ · L + ⅙ · W + ⅙ · I )
All components are normalized to a 0–100 scale within each city before being averaged, so that scores remain comparable across Paris and Mexico City despite differences in absolute levels. Exclusions deliberately bias the composite toward upstream planning choices instead of discretionary consumption*.

VI · Findings

Is planning associated with income?

This section reports a correlation between neighborhood planning quality and median income — not proof that better planning causes higher incomes. Wealthy neighborhoods may demand more planning, pay more taxes, attract more investment, or have been historically advantaged for reasons that have little to do with parks or transit lines.

Our central regression takes the form:

log( Median Incomei ) = α + β · Indexi + γ · Xi + εi

where i indexes neighborhoods, X contains controls (population, distance to center), run separately for each city. β is read as an association: a one-standard-deviation increase in the index is linked to a percentage change in median income, holding controls fixed.

What we cannot conclude from β alone

  • Reverse causality: high-income neighborhoods may demand — and pay for — better planning, not the other way around.
  • Omitted history: colonial legacies, state capacity, migration, housing law, and geography shape both income and the built environment over decades.
  • Simultaneity: planning and income are often co-produced; the regression captures a snapshot, not a mechanism.

With real 2022 Paris and 2020 Mexico cross-sections, the picture is more mixed than a simple “Paris stronger” story: bivariate correlations are modest in Paris and stronger in Mexico on our crowding proxy, while controlled regressions (population + distance to map center) leave the index coefficient small and statistically indistinguishable from zero in both cities — a reminder that snapshot associations are fragile.

Formal planning and money do not move in lockstep: in Paris, amenity-rich eastern districts can score high on our index while remaining relatively poor; in Mexico City, the index tracks crowding more tightly than controlled regressions suggest once population and centrality are held fixed.

One reading is that planning concentrates value: where the state has invested, land becomes scarce and expensive, and the wealthy capture it. Another is that planning is more substitutable in Mexico City: residents have built their own substitutes for parks, transit, and third spaces over decades, so formal versions matter less in the data. A third is that both stories are partly true — and that history, not index scores alone, explains which neighborhoods end up rich. We do not yet know which is closer to true.

VII · Debates

What the cities are arguing about now.

Gentrification

Who pays when a neighborhood gets nicer?

In both cities, the same paradox: improving urban planning quality — adding parks, restoring transit, pedestrianizing streets — raises rents and displaces the residents who waited longest for those improvements. Mexico City's Roma and Condesa neighborhoods, and Paris's 18th and 19th arrondissements, are textbook cases.

Monuments

What does a city remember in stone?

Mexico City's Avenida Reforma replaced its statue of Christopher Columbus with a sculpture honoring indigenous women in 2022. Paris is debating what to do with monuments to Colbert and other figures of empire. Urban planning is also a public memory project — and an unequal one.
