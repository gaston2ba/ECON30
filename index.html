I · Opening

Two cities, two grammars of progress.

Walk down the Boulevard Saint-Germain in Paris and you are walking inside a decision. The wide allée, the uniform façades, the angled intersections — all of it was drawn on a map in the 1850s by Baron Haussmann and stamped onto the city by imperial decree. Walk through Iztapalapa in Mexico City and you are walking inside a different kind of decision: the absence of one. The streets bend around hillsides. Houses rose first, sewers came later, deeds came later still. Both cities are the result of urban planning. One was planned by the state. The other was planned by everyone except the state.

If urban planning is one of the great engines of progress, then the two cities ask the same question in opposite voices: progress for whom?

This project asks whether we can measure the quality of urban planning at the neighborhood level, and whether that quality predicts how rich or poor a neighborhood is. We compare the 20 arrondissements of Paris with the 16 alcaldías of Mexico City — units of roughly the same scale, in cities that are, in almost every other respect, opposites.

The result, we hope, is a small contribution to a much older conversation: about who gets to draw the lines, who has to live inside them, and what spills across the boundary in either direction.

Trajectory I · Seine · 1850s onward

Paris — state theater of ordre et progrès.

Haussmann’s boulevards, sewers, and parks inscribed a choreography of prestige and policing. Narrative arcs — demolitions → spatial sorting → durable rent gradients — unfold in the Paris ribbon below.

Trajectory II · Valley of Mexico · 1880s Porfirian wave

Mexico City — Paris read through volcanoes.

Under Díaz, planners carved European boulevards through indigenous ground while informal colonias populares thickened at the rims. Parallel stories — ceremonial axes, scarcity of services, insurgent commons — unfold in the Mexico ribbon.

Everything else on this site — timelines, tessellated cartograms, regressions — hangs on this pairing: imperial boulevard grammars confronting low-income geography that refuses to pretend it is ornamental.

IV · Our Index

An index of urban planning quality.

To compare neighborhoods across two very different cities, we built an index of urban planning quality — a score from 0 to 100 that combines six measurable features of how well a neighborhood is built and served. Each component is, as best we can manage, upstream of income.

Use the checkmarks to include or exclude pillars from the composite score. Each pillar keeps its narrative sealed behind a Why? tab so the grid stays legible until you need the fine print.

The Index · Build Your Own 6 of 6 active
Park access
Measurement

Share of residents living within a roughly 10‑minute walk (~800 m) of a public park entrance, using network buffers rather than crows‑flight circles so footbridges and dead ends matter.

Rationale

Tree canopy and soft ground are expensive to retrofit; park proximity is a durable public investment that usually predates the wave of gentrificationNeighborhood ascent in rents and occupancy costs after amenities improve, sometimes displacing long-term renters. rents, making it useful as an upstream planning input.

Transit commute
Measurement

Median public‑transit journey time from each district to its historic employment pole (Île‑de‑France employment core for Paris; Zócalo–Reforma spine for CDMX), inverted so faster trips score higher.

Rationale

Network speed shapes labor‑market access; we avoid counting private vehicles because they track income more tightly than trains or BRT.

Productive density
Measurement

Residents per km² smoothed toward a planner‑defined band where infrastructure can still amortize fixed costs — neither ghost towers nor corrugated roof sprawl extremes score best.

Rationale

Urban economics treats density as throughput for agglomeration, but unmanaged crowding destroys service quality — we penalize tails.

Third spaces
Measurement

Libraries & municipally operated community centers per 10k residents — rooms where you can linger without buying lattes.

Rationale

These are legislated public third places, closer to planning decisions than cafés (which we treat as outcomes downstream of income).

Walkability
Measurement

Kilometers per km² of foot‑first streets plus verifiably porous plazas (no locked corporate atria).

Rationale

Walking fabric is politically cheap to advertise but unevenly enforced; our metric rewards continuous mesh, not billboard claims.

Basic infrastructure
Measurement

Share of streets with complete paving + storm drainage hookups, blended with PM2.5 readings as a proxy for combustion exposure on arterials.

Rationale

These are the least sexy line items in a budget but the first ones withheld from informalSettlements built ahead of formal cadastral recognition; services often arrive late or through political negotiation. settlements — so they signal state capacity.

VI · Findings

Does planning predict income?

Our central regression takes the form:

log( Median Incomei ) = α + β · Indexi + γ · Xi + εi

where i indexes neighborhoods, X contains controls (population, distance to center), run separately for each city.

We expect that the relationship between planning quality and income will be positive and strong in Paris, where the planned city is also the wealthy city — and weaker, perhaps even ambiguous, in Mexico City, where wealth has often grown in absence of formal planning (Polanco, Santa Fe) and where many high-quality public spaces exist in middle-income areas.

Preliminary · Paris
β ≈ +0.62
A one-standard-deviation increase in our index is associated with a 62% increase in median neighborhood income. Mock estimate — to be replaced with INSEE data.
Preliminary · Mexico City
β ≈ +0.28
The same one-standard-deviation increase predicts a 28% increase in median income — less than half the Paris effect. Mock estimate — to be replaced with INEGI data.

If the gap holds up, it points at something larger: the same planning amenity is worth more, in income terms, in a city where planning is the rule than in one where it is the exception.

One reading is that planning concentrates value: where the state has invested, land becomes scarce and expensive, and the wealthy capture it. Another reading is that planning is more substitutable in Mexico City: residents have built their own substitutes for parks, transit, and third spaces over decades, so the formal versions matter less. We do not yet know which is closer to true.

VII · Debates

What the cities are arguing about now.

Gentrification

Who pays when a neighborhood gets nicer?

In both cities, the same paradox: improving urban planning quality — adding parks, restoring transit, pedestrianizing streets — raises rents and displaces the residents who waited longest for those improvements. Mexico City's Roma and Condesa neighborhoods, and Paris's 18th and 19th arrondissements, are textbook cases.

Monuments

What does a city remember in stone?

Mexico City's Avenida Reforma replaced its statue of Christopher Columbus with a sculpture honoring indigenous women in 2022. Paris is debating what to do with monuments to Colbert and other figures of empire. Urban planning is also a public memory project — and an unequal one.
