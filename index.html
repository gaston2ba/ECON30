Paris

Paris

1853–1870

Haussmann redraws Paris

Wide boulevards, uniform façades, and networked sewers displace working-class blocks in the center. The state claims modernization; residents lose cheap housing.

1860

Annexation of the ring

Eleven communes join Paris; arrondissements XX are fixed. The map still used for census and planning is born.

1889

World’s Fair and Eiffel Tower

Industrial spectacle on the Champ de Mars. Iron and gas light signal a city confident in display.

1900

Métro opens

Line 1 links Porte de Vincennes to Porte Maillot. Transit becomes the armature of daily movement.

1925

Plan Voisin proposed

Le Corbusier’s tabula rasa for the Marais is rejected, but the dream of clearance persists in later estates.

1945–1973

Trente Glorieuses estates

Mass housing at the banlieueThe suburbs outside the Paris municipality, where much postwar public housing was built. edge. Center density falls; periphery towers rise.

1973

Périphérique completed

The ring road locks car traffic into a moat around the historic core.

2001

Bike and pedestrian turn

Successive mayors expand Vélib’, bus lanes, and school streets. The center softens slightly.

2014

Climate Paris

Anne Hidalgo targets diesel, parking, and heat islands. Green space becomes explicit policy.

2024

Olympic city, short-term edge

Games infrastructure and rental pressure test who benefits from global attention.

Mexico City

1876

Díaz and the imported blueprint

Porfirian elites hire foreign engineers for boulevards and drainage. The center is Europeanized; the periphery is ignored.

1885

Paseo de la Reforma

A ceremonial spine links the historic core to Chapultepec. Symbolic order precedes service equity.

1900

Drainage and Chapultepec

Tunnels begin to dry the basin; parks serve the west. Flooding risk shifts but does not vanish.

1910–1917

Revolution interrupts

Elite modernization pauses; migration and land politics reshape the valley.

1929–1950

Plano Regulador vs colonias

Official plans meet self-built peripheries. The formal city grows slower than need.

1950s

Industrial migration

Factories and barrios expand east and north. Services lag behind roofs.

1968

Metro and Tlatelolco

Line 1 opens as political violence scars the center. Transit promises modernity under tension.

1985

Earthquake exposes uneven safety

Building quality and rescue capacity vary sharply by neighborhood.

1990s–2010s

Centro redevelopment

GentrificationRising rents and new amenities in working-class areas, often displacing long-term residents. and cultural investment return to the core while peripheries still wait for water.

2016–2024

Cablebús and UTOPIAs

Peripheral integration projects attempt to close decades of service gaps.

Closing

Both cities concentrate planning quality in some districts and not others. On these maps, that spatial inequalityUnequal distribution of public goods and environmental burdens across neighborhoods in the same city. aligns with human development: where the UQI is low, HDI tends to be low as well.

They arrive there by different routes. Paris built universal networks early; inequality now shows up more in health and income than in piped water. Mexico City still encodes Porfirian and post-revolutionary gaps in infrastructure, with a wider spread in both UQI and HDI. This is not inequality of pavement alone. It is inequality of life.
