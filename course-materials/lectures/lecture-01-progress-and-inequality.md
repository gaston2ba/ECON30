## 1. Millennia of modest growth

### The world before 1800

Implied annual growth rate: 0.006% per year (only 0.031% even between 500 and 1700).

### The Malthusian trap

In 1798, Malthus argued in *An Essay on the Principle of Population* that **any improvement in living standards was temporary**:

- Better conditions → more children.
- More people → living standards fall back.
- Result: **millennia of near-zero growth in income per person.**

### The Malthusian model: predictions

The model makes sharp, testable predictions:

- **Living standards stay roughly constant.**
- **Higher productivity → more people, not richer people.** China in 1000 CE was the most technologically advanced civilization on Earth — gunpowder, the compass, paper money, cast iron, canal locks. Yet GDP per capita (~$600) was no higher than Europe's. It simply had *twice the population*.
- **Catastrophes could *raise* living standards.** The Black Death (1347–1351) killed ~40% of Europe's population. Real wages in England *doubled* within a generation (Clark, 2007). Land per worker rose, so the survivors were richer.

## 2. The miracle of progress

### Escaping the trap

The Malthusian trap held for **10,000+ years**. Then something changed.

- Around 1800, income per person began to rise — and *kept* rising.
- Population grew too, but income grew **faster**.
- The hockey stick of progress was born.

What broke the trap? That is one of the biggest questions in economics — and a central theme of this course.

### Falling child mortality and life expectancy

- Child mortality fell globally.
- Mortality first fell for children, then adults (example: France).

### Quote — Benjamin Franklin

> "The rapid progress true Science now makes, occasions my regretting sometimes that I was born so soon."

## 3. Drivers of progress

### The explosive growth of collective intelligence

An iPhone contains components from dozens of countries, thousands of suppliers, and millions of hours of accumulated knowledge.

### Four revolutions that changed everything

*Adapted from Thomas Friedman's phase-change metaphor.*

#### Revolution 1 — The Neolithic Revolution (~10,000 BCE)

For 200,000 years, small bands of hunter-gatherers were scattered across the globe — ideas, people, and goods moved slowly, knowledge was oral, and living standards barely changed. Then climate change triggered the first **phase change**:

- Increasing seasonal variability forced hunter-gatherers to store food and settle down.
- Storage → sedentism → agriculture (Matranga, "The Ant and the Grasshopper").
- Agriculture → surplus → cities → states → writing — and **inequality**.
- But growth remained slow (~0.006% per year).

#### Revolution 2 — The Intellectual Revolution (~500 BCE)

Writing, cities, and trade networks created the conditions for a revolution in ideas.

- Greek philosophy and democracy, Confucianism, Buddhism, the Hebrew prophets — all emerged roughly simultaneously across unconnected civilizations.
- For the first time, humans asked systematic questions about governance, ethics, and the nature of the world.
- New institutions: codified law, formal education, organized religion.
- But the Malthusian trap still held — most people remained poor.

#### Revolution 3 — The Industrial Revolution (~1760 AD)

Ideas began to **flow**: books, newspapers, telegraph, telephone, internet.

- Capital and people flowed across borders.
- The Industrial Revolution, globalization, the information age.
- The hockey-stick growth we saw earlier happened here (growth above 1% per year).
- Humanity finally escaped the Malthusian trap.

#### Revolution 4 — The Digital / AI Revolution (since ~2022)

- AI in your watch, your car, your toaster.
- Intelligence becoming ambient and ubiquitous.
- Are we one step away from another fundamental transformation?

### Quote — Sam Altman

> "After thousands of years of compounding scientific discovery and technological progress, we have figured out how to melt sand, add some impurities, arrange it with astonishing precision at extraordinarily tiny scale into computer chips, run energy through it, and end up with systems capable of creating increasingly capable artificial intelligence."

### Quote — Demis Hassabis

> "Computers are just bits of sand and copper. Why should these combine to do anything? I mean, it's absurd! The electrons move around and then that creates an AI system that can defeat a Go master? Why should that be possible? This is beyond evolutionary coincidence. We can build electron microscopes and interrogate reality down to the most minute level. We can build systems that detect black holes colliding more than a billion years ago. I mean, what is this? What the hell is going on here?"

## 4. Who benefits from progress?

### Progress for whom?

The fruits of progress are not always equally distributed.

- Who builds these technologies? Who benefits from them?
- The same forces that create extraordinary wealth can leave millions behind.
- **Progress and inequality are twins born together** — and understanding both is the mission of this course.

### Reversal of fortunes

Before colonialism, some of the **wealthiest regions** in the world were in Asia, Africa, and the Americas.

- Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson (2002): countries that were **rich in 1500 are poor today**, and vice versa.
- Colonizers imposed **extractive institutions** in prosperous, densely populated regions — designed to exploit, not to develop.
- Meanwhile, **settler colonies** (e.g., US, Australia) received inclusive institutions that fostered long-run growth.
- The result: a dramatic **reversal** in the global income distribution over 500 years.

Referenced figures in the deck: two panels from Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson (2002); Brazil growth chart from Lambaisa & Palma (2026); settler mortality vs. institutions (AJR 2001); GDP per capita by country (World Bank); correlation and IV-causal panels on institutions and prosperity (AJR 2024 Nobel Prize Lectures).

## 5. Progress studies and this course

### Progress studies

Progress is **not automatic**. Collison & Cowen (2019) call for a new field dedicated to understanding why progress happens — and how to speed it up.

- Progress has been **uneven**. Some ecosystems generate orders-of-magnitude more progress.
- **Why?** The relevant knowledge is **fragmented** across economics, history, sociology, management, science policy — no field owns the question.

Progress studies asks: How should we fund science? Train talent? Design organizations? Structure incentives? The goal is not just to understand — but to **intervene**.

### Quote — Joel Mokyr

> "The idea that humans should and could work consciously to make the world a better place for themselves and for generations to come is by and large one that emerged in the two centuries between Christopher Columbus and Isaac Newton. Of course, just believing that progress could be brought about is not enough — one must bring it about. The modern world began when people resolved to do so."

## 6. Your capstone project

### Interactive components of the course

- **Assignments** (30% of grade) — weekly assignments that build toward the capstone, including peer evaluation.
- **Class participation** (30% of grade) — attendance, discussion, asking good questions.
- **Capstone draft** (10% of grade) — mid-quarter draft, submitted for peer feedback.
- **Capstone project** (30% of grade) — an original research project on an aspect of progress that fascinates you.

### The capstone project

An original research project on **an aspect of progress that fascinates you**:

- Each week you produce something that builds toward your final project.
- Your capstone should combine:
  - **Quantitative analysis and data.**
  - A **qualitative, personal component.**
  - **Clear, compelling writing** that a general audience can enjoy.
- Your classmates will **peer-grade** each other, so write for a broad audience.

### What makes a great capstone?

1. Focuses on a very specific, important technology, policy, organization, … (examples: microscopes, dating, …).
2. Takes a long-run view on the history and future of that aspect — e.g., how have **microscopes' resolutions** evolved and how has that changed their use cases? How has **courting / dating** changed over centuries, where do people meet, how does dating differ by class, and what's next?
3. Highlights how progress and **inequality** intersect in this domain — e.g., who has access to breakthrough innovations and what use-cases are they used for? Which groups are struggling to find partners to date; have public spheres been eroded at the cost of dating opportunities?
4. Combines existing evidence with original data (e.g., interviews, web-scraping, archives).
5. Has a **qualitative personal component.**
6. Is **well-written / designed** and **interactive** (e.g., interactive HTML, video, …).
