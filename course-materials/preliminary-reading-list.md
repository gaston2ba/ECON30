# Preliminary Reading List

This is a first-pass bibliography for next week. The goal is not to read everything at once, but to build a balanced list with theory, Paris, Mexico City, and comparative/methods coverage.

## Priority shortlist for next week

### Theory and conceptual frame

1. Ray Oldenburg, *The Great Good Place*.
   Why it matters: the classic statement on "third places" and informal public sociability beyond home and work.

2. Jane Jacobs, *The Death and Life of Great American Cities*.
   Why it matters: gives language for street life, mixed use, density, and the social importance of everyday public interaction.

3. Jan Gehl, *Life Between Buildings*.
   Why it matters: helps connect physical urban design to how people actually use space.

### Paris / Haussmann

4. Stephen V. Ward, "Planning law, power, and practice: Haussmann in Paris (1853-1870)."
   Why it matters: useful for understanding the legal and political machinery behind redesign, not just the visual outcome.

5. Yonah Freemark, Laura Bliss, and Lawrence J. Vale, "Housing Haussmann's Paris: the politics and legacy of Second Empire redevelopment."
   Why it matters: adds a more recent discussion of redevelopment, housing, and the debate over displacement.

6. David P. Jordan, *Transforming Paris: The Life and Labors of Baron Haussmann*.
   Why it matters: a foundational historical account of Haussmannization and its political-economic ambitions.

### Mexico City / Porfiriato

7. Emily Wakild, "Naturalizing Modernity: Urban Parks, Public Gardens and Drainage Projects in Porfirian Mexico City."
   Why it matters: one of the clearest direct links between public space, nature, and modernization in your Mexico City case.

8. Claudia Agostoni, *Monuments of Progress: Modernization and Public Health in Mexico City, 1876-1910*.
   Why it matters: helps situate redesign within public health, hygiene, and state-building projects.

9. Mauricio Tenorio-Trillo, *I Speak of the City: Mexico City at the Turn of the Twentieth Century*.
   Why it matters: strong contextual reading on elite imaginaries, modernity, and urban self-presentation in Mexico City.

### Comparative and methods

10. Heath Schenker, *Melodramatic Landscapes: Urban Parks in the Nineteenth Century*.
    Why it matters: especially valuable because it puts Paris and Mexico City into a shared comparative frame through park history.

## Secondary or extension readings

11. "Inequitable distribution of green public space in Mexico City: an environmental injustice case."
    Why it matters: useful if you want to connect the historical story to present-day spatial inequality.

12. James Elliot, *The City in Maps: Urban Mapping to 1900*.
    Why it matters: a methods-oriented source for treating maps as historical evidence rather than simple illustration.

## Suggested reading order

1. Oldenburg or Jacobs for the conceptual language.
2. Ward plus Jordan for the Paris case.
3. Wakild plus Agostoni for the Mexico City case.
4. Schenker once the two city cases are clearer.

## Questions to keep in mind while reading

- Who is the imagined public for each space?
- What kinds of access are expanded, and which are still restricted?
- How closely are public space projects tied to commerce, sanitation, prestige, or policing?
- Do the authors describe these spaces as democratizing, disciplinary, exclusionary, or some mix of all three?
