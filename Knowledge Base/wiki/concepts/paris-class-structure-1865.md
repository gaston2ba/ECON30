# Paris Class Structure, 1865

A single empirical snapshot, halfway through Haussmann's renovation, that the project uses as the baseline class portrait of the city being remade.

## The numbers

According to a census conducted by the City of Paris in 1865 (cited in `raw/Paris/catalog.md`, dataset 2):

| Class | Share of Paris population | Approximate count | Defining criterion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indigent | 42 % | ~780,000 | Too poor to be taxed |
| Lower-middle class | 17 % | — | Rents under 250 francs |
| Upper-middle class | 32 % | — | Rents 250–1,500 francs |
| Wealthy | 3 % | ~50,000 | Rents over 1,500 francs |

The total implied population is approximately 1.85 million, consistent with the 1866 INSEE census.

## What this snapshot tells us

- **Paris in the middle of "embellissement" was overwhelmingly poor.** Six in ten Parisians were either indigent or lower-middle. The renovation was not happening in a wealthy city being made wealthier; it was happening in a poor city.
- **The wealthy were a small minority.** Three percent. Whatever the renovation produced for them — wider boulevards, monumental perspectives, the Bois de Boulogne, the new department stores documented in [[zola-au-bonheur-des-dames]] — was produced for that 3 %, in residential terms. The middle 32 % benefited as a secondary market.
- **The "indigent" category is the same population Hugo names *les misérables*.** It is the population Zola will document, after relegation to the periphery, in [[zola-lassommoir]]. It is the population that died in the 1832 and 1849 cholera epidemics. It is the population the renovation was about to rearrange.

## Use in the project

- **Figure 1 of the Paris pipeline.** A bar or pie chart of these four shares is the simplest, most striking opening visualization. It is item 1 in the catalog's suggested figure pipeline. **Rendered** as the left panel of [[figure-01-class-structure]] (`figures/figure-01-paris-1865-mexico-city-1895-class-structure.png`), with the 6% residual carried explicitly as an `Unattributed` bar rather than being silently rescaled.
- **Pairs with [[mexico-city-class-structure-1900]].** The Porfiriato counterpart is the c. 1895–1900 occupational structure of the Federal District (~60–65% *jornaleros* and domestic servants per the 1895 census). The two charts side by side establish the "starting condition" for both halves of the comparison.
- **Anchor for any later display of present-day income data.** The current INSEE Filosofi map, displayed alongside the 1865 numbers, is what makes the persistence claim feel concrete: this same population, redistributed in space, is still there.

## Source quality and caveats

- The 1865 City of Paris census is a one-off municipal exercise, not part of the regular national census series. The 42 / 17 / 32 / 3 split appears in `raw/Paris/catalog.md` cited via Wikipedia / Second Empire summaries; for publication, the underlying primary should be checked against the *Annuaire statistique de la Ville de Paris* of the period.
- "Indigent" is defined administratively (untaxed), not by a modern poverty line. Direct comparison with present-day INSEE poverty rates is suggestive, not exact.
