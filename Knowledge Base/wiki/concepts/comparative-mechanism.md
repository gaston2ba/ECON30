# Comparative Mechanism — Haussmann / Porfiriato

The project's central thesis: that Haussmann's Paris (1853–1870) and Díaz's Mexico City (1876–1911) instantiate the *same urban-modernization mechanism*, that this mechanism is empirically auditable on a parallel set of historical and present-day datasets, and that the resulting class geography is still legible in 2020-resolution data in both cities — with one structural difference (race) that the Mexico City case forces the model to absorb.

## The mechanism, in five steps

1. **A modernizing state appropriates a universal vocabulary.** "Sanitation," "circulation," "beauty," "progress." Haussmann uses it; Díaz, modeling himself on Haussmann (Tenorio-Trillo 1996), uses the same vocabulary translated into Spanish — *higiene, ornato, orden y progreso*.
2. **A real public-health crisis is mobilized as the entry point.** Cholera in Paris (1832, 1849); typhus and cholera in Mexico City (1880s–1900s). The mortality is real and concentrated in poor neighborhoods. See [[sanitation-as-pretext]].
3. **State authority is exercised through demolition and infrastructure.** Wide boulevards, monumental buildings, water and drainage systems, parks. The investment footprint is geographically uneven — concentrated in central and (especially) wealthier zones.
4. **Working-class (and, in Mexico City, indigenous) residents are displaced.** Out from the center in Paris; both displaced from the center / east and *out-attracted* by the new western colonias in Mexico City. See [[spatial-displacement]].
5. **The resulting geography hardens.** Real estate, amenity provision, transit access, and political representation all subsequently track the spatial pattern the renovation produced. A century later, the maps still show the same axis.

The IRIS / AGEB pairing in the modern row is what makes the persistence claim measurable at comparable resolution. See [[iris-and-ageb]].

## The structural divergence: race

The mechanism produces non-identical outputs in the two cases. Paris in 1865–1872 was class-stratified but not racially stratified; the displaced population and the elite population were the same nominal ethnic group. Mexico City was both class- and race-stratified, and Porfirian urbanism *spatialized* the racial hierarchy in a way the colonial *traza* had not done at the same resolution. See [[racial-stratification-mexico-city]].

This means the Mexico City inequality map today is not a neat radial gradient (the way the Paris map approximately is); it is a mosaic (per Duhau & Giglia 2008) in which class and race overlap but produce more fragmented spatial patterns. The model has to explain both the parallel and this divergence.

## What would falsify the thesis

The project should keep a clear list of findings that would weaken the central claim:

- If the cholera / typhus mortality maps coincided closely with the actual demolition and investment footprints, the [[sanitation-as-pretext]] reading would be weakened — the renovation would have been responding proportionally to the disease burden.
- If the 1851→1872 Paris population shift turned out to be uniform across arrondissements (controlling for the 1860 boundary expansion), the displacement claim would weaken.
- If present-day INSEE Filosofi income at IRIS resolution did not show the expected west/east gradient, the persistence claim would fail — the geography would not have hardened.
- If the third-place per-capita measures came back flat or counter-axis, the [[third-place-test]] would not support the universal-public-space-failure reading.
- If indigenous-population-share at AGEB resolution showed *no* correlation with income or with western-elite vs. eastern-poor location, the racial layer would not be a separable variable and the project's modification of the Haussmann template would be misplaced.

The project's epistemic posture should be that the central thesis is well-supported by current scholarship (Harvey, Freemark/Bliss/Vale, da Costa Meyer for Paris; Johns, Tenorio-Trillo, Piccato, Davis, Duhau & Giglia for Mexico City) but that each empirical step in the parallel must be checked rather than assumed.
