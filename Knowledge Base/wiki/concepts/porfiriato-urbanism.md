# Porfiriato Urbanism

The remaking of Mexico City under Porfirio Díaz (1876–1911), explicitly modeled on Haussmann's Paris: cutting and paving of grand boulevards (Paseo de la Reforma extension, Avenida Juárez), expansion of central drainage and water infrastructure, the *Gran Canal del Desagüe* (1900), monumental construction (Palacio Postal, Palacio de Bellas Artes, Monumento a la Independencia), and the planned development of new western elite *colonias* (Juárez, Roma, Condesa, San Rafael, Santa María la Ribera) on land formerly held by indigenous communities or used for low-status purposes.

## Stated rationale

Per Tenorio-Trillo (1996) and Johns (1997), Díaz's urbanism was framed in the rhetoric of *orden y progreso*: scientific modernization, sanitation, European civilizational standards, and Mexico's display to the world (the World's Fairs, the centenario of 1910). The same vocabulary as Haussmann.

## What it actually did, empirically

Per the catalog (`raw/MexicoCity/catalog.md`) and the listed scholarship (Johns 1997, Piccato 2001, Rodríguez Kuri 1996, Davis 1994):

- **Reorganized population westward.** INEGI 1895 and 1910 censuses show the new western *colonias* growing rapidly while the central indigenous and eastern working-class barrios stagnated or were displaced — the directional equivalent of Paris center-to-periphery, but with western *attraction* of elites rather than only central *expulsion* of workers. See [[spatial-displacement]].
- **Concentrated public-works investment in elite zones.** The *Memorias de Obras Públicas* (Secretaría de Fomento) document the geographic distribution of pesos spent on paving, drainage, and lighting; the western *colonias* received a disproportionate share.
- **Used sanitation arguments to justify intervention in poor neighborhoods.** Typhus and cholera mortality data from the *Boletín del Consejo Superior de Salubridad* concentrated in central and eastern barrios, exactly the zones targeted for "renovation." See [[sanitation-as-pretext]].
- **Encoded racial stratification into spatial form.** Unlike Paris, Porfirian Mexico City was racially as well as economically stratified; the western *colonias* were explicitly Europeanizing in their architectural language and in their population. See [[racial-stratification-mexico-city]].

## Where it diverges from Haussmann

Two key differences the project should keep visible:

1. **Mosaic, not center/periphery.** Duhau & Giglia (2008) argue that the Porfirian (and post-Porfirian) order produced a fragmented mosaic of class enclaves rather than a clean radial gradient. The Mexico City inequality map today is not the simple inverse of Paris's.
2. **Race.** Haussmann's Paris was class-stratified; Díaz's Mexico City was class- *and* race-stratified, and the racial layer is the variable with no Paris equivalent. INEGI's AGEB-level indigenous-population data is the modern empirical handle on this.

## Why it matters for the project

Porfiriato urbanism is the *test case* for the project's central comparative claim (see [[comparative-mechanism]]): the Haussmann template was self-consciously imported, the same universalizing rhetoric was deployed, and a measurable persistence of the resulting class geography is visible in 2020 AGEB data. The fact that Mexico City's geography is racially as well as economically stratified is the modification the model has to absorb.
