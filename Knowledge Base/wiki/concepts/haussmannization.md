# Haussmannization

The Second Empire transformation of Paris (1853–1870) directed by Georges-Eugène Haussmann under Napoléon III: large-scale demolition of central neighborhoods, cutting of wide straight boulevards, expansion to twenty arrondissements via the 1860 annexation, construction of an integrated water and sewer system, and a citywide network of parks and squares designed by Adolphe Alphand.

## Stated rationale

Three justifications, all of them used at the time:

1. **Sanitation.** The 1832 and 1849 cholera epidemics had killed disproportionately in the dense central working-class neighborhoods. Demolition and the new sewer/water system were framed as public health. (See [[sanitation-as-pretext]] for the case that mortality data was used to legitimize a class-targeted intervention.)
2. **Aesthetics and circulation.** Wide boulevards, monumental perspectives, and a connected park network as a modern capital fit for empire and commerce.
3. **Order.** Wide straight streets are harder to barricade. Hugo's account of the 1832 uprising in *Les Misérables* documents the older street pattern this purpose targeted.

## What it actually did, empirically

Per the catalog (`raw/Paris/catalog.md`) and the article literature (Freemark/Bliss/Vale 2022, da Costa Meyer 2022, Harvey 2003):

- **Displaced central working-class residents outward.** The INSEE 1851–1872 censuses show the central arrondissements emptying and the peripheral ones (especially the post-1860 northern and eastern annexed communes) filling — see [[spatial-displacement]].
- **Concentrated investment in central and western Paris.** The new boulevards, parks, and commercial blocks were not evenly distributed; the western arrondissements got the larger share, an asymmetry the present-day income map still reflects.
- **Built a commercial geography.** The wide boulevards and consolidated blocks were the physical precondition for the modern department store (Boucicaut's Le Bon Marché, fictionalized in Zola's *Au Bonheur des Dames*) and for a new bourgeois consumer public.
- **Produced a class-visible street.** Baudelaire's "Eyes of the Poor" registers, in 1869, the new boulevard café as a space the poor can see into but not enter — direct literary evidence for the [[third-place-test]].

## Why this is the project's anchor concept

Haussmannization is the single best-documented case of a 19th-century urban transformation that explicitly framed itself in universal language (sanitation, beauty, modernity) while reorganizing urban space in ways that produced enduring class geography. The project's argument is that the *same logic* operated under Díaz in Mexico City — see [[porfiriato-urbanism]] and [[comparative-mechanism]].
