# Sanitation as Pretext

A concept the project uses to describe a recurring move in 19th-century urban modernization: real epidemic mortality data, concentrated in poor neighborhoods, is used as the public justification for demolitions and infrastructure projects whose actual geography of investment and displacement tracks class (and, in Mexico City, race) more closely than it tracks disease.

## The mechanism

1. An epidemic — cholera in Paris (1832, 1849), typhus and cholera in Mexico City (1880s–1900s) — kills disproportionately in dense, low-income central neighborhoods.
2. Public-health authorities map mortality by neighborhood. The maps are real and the inequality in mortality is real.
3. State authority is mobilized for a *sanitation* intervention whose stated goal is universal disease prevention.
4. The intervention's actual footprint — demolition zones, new boulevards, drainage and water investment, park placement — is not allocated proportional to disease burden alone. It is allocated in ways that also displace working-class residents, raise central land values, and concentrate amenity investment in (or adjacent to) elite zones.
5. The post-intervention geography is then defended as scientifically necessary because the pre-intervention mortality data was real.

The trick is that step 1 is true. The mortality maps are not invented. The pretext claim is not that there was no epidemic; it is that the *spatial selection* of the response was driven by political economy as much as by epidemiology.

## Empirical evidence in the project

**Paris.** Cholera mortality data by arrondissement from the *Journal de la Société Statistique de Paris* (1865, via Numdam, listed in `raw/Paris/catalog.md`) overlaid on Haussmann demolition zones is the empirical test. Where mortality is high but demolition is light, or where demolition is heavy but mortality is moderate, is direct evidence for the pretext reading. Rosenthal (Caltech) is the cited contemporary scholar on this connection.

**Mexico City.** Typhus and cholera mortality by barrio from the *Boletín del Consejo Superior de Salubridad* (1880s–1900s, Archivo Histórico de la Secretaría de Salud, listed in `raw/MexicoCity/catalog.md`) plays the same role. The same overlay — disease map vs. *Memorias de Obras Públicas* investment map — is the test. Piccato (2001) *City of Suspects* argues that this sanitation-and-social-control logic was central to Porfirian governance.

## Why both cases together matter

The fact that the same rhetorical move appears in both Haussmann's Paris and Díaz's Mexico City — and in the second case explicitly modeled on the first — is part of the evidence for [[comparative-mechanism]]: the universalizing public-health language is a portable governance technology, not a one-off accident of Second Empire France.
