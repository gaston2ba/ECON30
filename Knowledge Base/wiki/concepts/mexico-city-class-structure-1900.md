# Mexico City Class Structure, c. 1895–1900

The Mexican counterpart to the [[paris-class-structure-1865]] snapshot: a baseline portrait of the Federal District population in the middle of the Porfirian transformation, used as the anchor figure for the Mexico City side of the project.

## The numbers

The 1895 census — the first methodologically modern Mexican census, conducted under Díaz — was the first to systematically record occupation. Per `raw/MexicoCity/catalog.md` (dataset 2):

- Roughly **60–65 % of the Federal District's population were classified as *jornaleros* (day laborers) and domestic servants.**

The catalog directs the project to extract finer breakdowns from the published census summaries (URL: INEGI digitized historical census, document 702825427952) to build a class structure chart parallel to the Paris one.

## What the snapshot tells us

- **The Federal District in the middle of *orden y progreso* was overwhelmingly working-class.** The 60–65 % *jornaleros* / domestic servants share is the directional equivalent of Paris's 42 % indigent + 17 % lower-middle (≈ 59 %) in 1865. The two cities, on the eve of and during their respective renovations, had broadly comparable class profiles at the bottom.
- **Occupation is the recorded variable, not income or rent.** The Mexican classification is *what people did for a living*, while the Paris 1865 split was *what people paid in rent*. The two are correlated but not identical, and any side-by-side bar chart should label the underlying variable honestly rather than implying a single "class" measure.
- **Race is a missing dimension here that matters.** The 1895 census did not consistently record indigenous status in a way that lets us split the *jornaleros* category, but the qualitative literature (Johns 1997, Piccato 2001) and modern AGEB-level indigenous-population data make clear that the bottom of the occupational distribution in 1900 was disproportionately indigenous. This is what [[racial-stratification-mexico-city]] tracks.

## Use in the project

- **Figure 1 of the Mexico City pipeline.** A bar chart of the c. 1900 occupational structure is item 1 in the catalog's suggested figure pipeline and is designed to sit immediately beside the Paris 1865 chart. **Partially rendered** as the right panel of [[figure-01-class-structure]]: only the jornaleros + domestic-servants bar (60–65%) is filled, with the artisan / middle / elite bars left explicitly blank to expose the data gap. Filling them is priority 1.2 of [[data-acquisition-checklist]].
- **Pre-Porfirian "before" complement.** Guillermo Prieto's *Memorias de mis tiempos* (1876, listed in the catalog's literature section) is the qualitative pre-Díaz baseline. Together with the 1895 census data they let one show both *what was there before* and *who was there during* the Porfirian transformation.
- **Anchor for present-day comparison.** The 2020 AGEB-level income and CONEVAL deprivation data, displayed against this baseline, are what carry the persistence claim — that the bottom 60–65 % of 1900 has a clearly identifiable spatial heir today (Iztapalapa, Tláhuac, Milpa Alta, parts of Gustavo A. Madero).

## Source quality and caveats

- The 60–65 % figure is approximate; for a publication-quality bar chart the exact INEGI-published occupational categories from 1895/1900 should be transcribed individually rather than collapsed.
- The geographic extent of the "Federal District" in 1895 differs from the modern 16-alcaldía boundary; population totals are not directly comparable across the boundary changes of 1928 and 2016.
- The Porfirian state had a strong incentive to demonstrate "progress" in its own census reporting; class categories should be read with that political-economy of statistics in mind.
