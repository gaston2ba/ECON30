# Summary — Hugo, *Les Misérables* (1862)

The raw files comprise the complete English Project Gutenberg edition plus three of the five French volumes (*Tome I — Fantine*, *Tome II — Cosette*, *Tome IV — L'Idylle rue Plumet et l'épopée rue Saint-Denis*). Tomes III (*Marius*) and V (*Jean Valjean*) are not included in `raw/`.

## What the novel is

Victor Hugo's panoramic 1862 novel, set largely in Paris between 1815 and 1832 — i.e. before Haussmann's renovation. It follows the convict Jean Valjean from his release through his pursuit by Inspector Javert, the rescue of the orphan Cosette, and the June 1832 Paris uprising. Hugo intercuts the narrative with long historical and topographical digressions — chapters on the Battle of Waterloo, on the Paris sewers, on argot, on the geography of specific neighborhoods.

## Why it matters for the project

*Les Misérables* is the project's literary record of pre-Haussmann Paris — the city of narrow streets, dense central poverty, and improvised barricades that Haussmann's plan would explicitly target:

- **Topography of pre-renovation Paris.** Hugo's neighborhood descriptions (the old Quartier Saint-Michel, the rue Plumet, the rue Saint-Denis, the central market quarters) document the street network that Haussmann would shortly demolish. Read alongside the David Rumsey 1850s maps in `raw/Paris/catalog.md` for georeferenced overlay.
- **Class structure on the eve of renovation.** The novel's central population — *les misérables*, the urban poor of central Paris — is exactly the demographic that the 1865 census would soon count as the 42% indigent (see [[paris-class-structure-1865]]) and that the renovation would push outward (see [[spatial-displacement]]).
- **The barricade as a political reading of the old street pattern.** The 1832 insurrection chapters in Tome IV explain why Haussmann's wide, straight, militarily indefensible boulevards were not an accident: the renovation's strategic purpose was, in part, to make the kind of barricade Hugo describes impossible. This is one of the project's bridges between the official aesthetic / sanitation justification and the political-economic one.

## Use in the project

- Quotation from Tome IV for the political reading of boulevard width.
- Topographic detail from Tomes I–II for any "before" comparison with post-Haussmann street geometry.
- Period-eye class texture as a complement to *L'Assommoir*: Hugo gives the central, pre-renovation poor; Zola gives the same population after relegation to the periphery.
