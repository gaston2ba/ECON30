# Summary — Zola, *L'Assommoir* (1877)

The raw file is the Project Gutenberg French-language edition of *L'Assommoir*, the seventh novel in Émile Zola's *Rougon-Macquart* cycle, first serialized 1876 and published in volume in 1877.

## What the novel is

A naturalist account of working-class life in the Goutte-d'Or neighborhood of Paris's 18th arrondissement — the northern periphery into which Haussmann's demolitions pushed displaced central-Paris workers. The novel follows Gervaise Macquart, a laundress, from her arrival in Paris through a brief period of stability, the collapse of her marriage to the roofer Coupeau, alcoholism, and destitution. The title refers to the cheap distillery (*assommoir*) where the workers drink themselves to ruin.

## Why it matters for the project

*L'Assommoir* is the most important literary primary source for the lived geography produced by [[haussmannization]]:

- **Setting.** The novel is anchored in the very arrondissements (18th, around Goutte-d'Or, rue de la Goutte-d'Or, rue des Poissonniers) that absorbed displaced workers from the central renovation zones. It is the literary record of the post-Haussmann periphery.
- **Class.** Every named character is working-class: laundresses, roofers, blacksmiths, chain-makers, *jornaliers* in all but name. Zola's documentary method (he kept notebooks of slang and trade vocabulary) produces a textual census of the bottom 60% of the city missing from official data.
- **Mechanism.** The novel shows how spatial relegation, precarious wages, and the *assommoir* economy interact to produce a mortality and immiseration trajectory — exactly the human content of the population-shift figures in the [[paris-catalog]].

## Pairings

Pairs with Federico Gamboa's *Santa* (1903) on the Mexico City side: same naturalist method, same trajectory of a working-class woman through the spatial economy of a "modernizing" capital.
