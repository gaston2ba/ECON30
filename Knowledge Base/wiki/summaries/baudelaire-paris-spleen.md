# Summary — Baudelaire, *Paris Spleen* (English translation)

The raw file is the Project Gutenberg edition of Charles Baudelaire's *Petits Poèmes en Prose* (translated by Arthur Symons, 1913 London edition; collection itself published posthumously in 1869). Fifty short prose poems set in mid-nineteenth-century Paris, written during and immediately after Haussmann's demolitions.

## The poem the project relies on: "The Eyes of the Poor" (IV)

The single most-cited piece for the project. The narrator and his lover sit outside a "new café at the corner of a new boulevard, still littered with plaster and already displaying proudly its unfinished splendours" — i.e., the literal physical scene of Haussmann's [[haussmannization]] in progress. A poor father with two ragged children stands across the road and stares at the café's gaslit luxury. The father's eyes say *how beautiful it is*; the boy's eyes say *but that is a house which only people who are not like us can enter*; the lover, by contrast, asks the narrator to have the head waiter send the poor family away.

This poem is the project's core literary evidence that the new boulevards and cafés of Haussmann's Paris produced a *visible* class boundary at the level of street furniture: the very public space the renovation claimed to democratize is experienced by Baudelaire's poor family as a threshold they cannot cross. It is the literary anchor for [[third-place-test]] (whether "public" Haussmannian space was actually public) and pairs directly with the 1865 census numbers in [[paris-class-structure-1865]].

## Other useful pieces

The collection contains several additional poems with relevant urban material — windows looking into poor apartments ("Windows," V), encounters with beggars and street performers, meditations on the crowd. The "Eyes of the Poor" is the central one, but the collection as a whole supplies the period-eye texture of Second Empire Paris from a writer who watched the renovation happen.

## Use in the project

- Quotation in any introduction or framing section that needs a single image of the Haussmann boulevard's class geometry.
- Counterweight to the official sanitation/aesthetic justification for the renovation — Baudelaire saw the new boulevard and registered exclusion, not progress.
- Pairs in the Mexico City dossier with Ángel de Campo's chronicles of poor barrios under Porfirian "modernization."
