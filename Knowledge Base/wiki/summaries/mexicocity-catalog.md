# Summary — Mexico City / Porfiriato Source Catalog

`raw/MexicoCity/catalog.md` is a curated meta-source for the Mexico City side of the project. Its structure intentionally mirrors `raw/Paris/catalog.md` so each Paris claim has a Mexico City counterpart and the comparative mechanism stays visible.

## What the catalog contains

- **Mexican literature for the Porfiriato.** Federico Gamboa's *Santa* (1903) — the canonical Porfirian realist novel, equivalent to Zola's *L'Assommoir*; Ángel de Campo's chronicles of poor barrios (1890s–1900s); Manuel Gutiérrez Nájera's *Cuentos frágiles* (1883) as the modernista voice of the elite; Guillermo Prieto's *Memorias de mis tiempos* (1876) as the pre-Porfiriato "before" document.
- **Historical datasets (1857–1910).** INEGI censuses 1895 / 1900 / 1910 (the first methodologically modern Mexican censuses, Porfirian state-building output); occupational structure data from the 1895 census showing roughly 60–65% of the Federal District as *jornaleros* and domestic servants; cuartel-level population records pre- and post-Porfiriato; typhus and cholera mortality by barrio from the *Boletín del Consejo Superior de Salubridad*; *Memorias de Obras Públicas* documenting public investment by neighborhood.
- **Present-day inequality datasets.** INEGI AGEB-level income and poverty (Censo 2020) — Mexico's IRIS equivalent; CONEVAL's multidimensional poverty / social-lag index; Datos Abiertos CDMX for green spaces, alcaldía/colonia boundaries, social housing, and *valor catastral*; SHF property price index by colonia from 2005; INEGI Encuesta Intercensal 2015 / Censo 2020 indigenous-population data by AGEB.
- **Spatial / GIS sources.** Mapoteca Digital Orozco y Berra (georeferenced historical maps 1850s–1920s); Casasola Archive photographs (INAH Fototeca Nacional, 1895–1920); OpenStreetMap Mexico City.
- **Key academic literature.** Johns (1997) *The City of Mexico in the Age of Díaz*; Tenorio-Trillo (1996) *Mexico at the World's Fairs*; Piccato (2001) *City of Suspects*; Rodríguez Kuri (1996); Aguilar & Ward (2003); Duhau & Giglia (2008) *Las reglas del desorden*; CONEVAL (2021); Davis (1994) *Urban Leviathan*; Leal et al. (2019).

## Argument the catalog is built to support

The Mexico City dossier supports four claims that parallel the Paris ones, plus one that does not:

1. **Pre-Porfirian Mexico City was poor and racially stratified.** Prieto's memoir and the 1895 occupational census establish the population Díaz was about to remake.
2. **Epidemic mortality concentrated in poor central and eastern barrios.** Typhus / cholera data from the *Boletín del Consejo Superior de Salubridad* parallels the Paris cholera evidence — see [[sanitation-as-pretext]].
3. **Porfirian "modernization" displaced indigenous and working-class residents while concentrating investment in western elite colonias.** *Memorias de Obras Públicas* and 1895/1910 census comparisons make this visible — see [[spatial-displacement]] and [[porfiriato-urbanism]].
4. **The Porfirian geography persists in 2020 inequality data.** AGEB-level income, CONEVAL deprivation, and SHF prices since 2005 show Polanco/Las Lomas vs. Iztapalapa/Tláhuac as the same axis Díaz reinforced.
5. **Mexico City's inequality is racially stratified in a way Paris's is not.** Duhau & Giglia argue the spatial order is not center/periphery but a mosaic of enclaves; INEGI indigenous-population data by AGEB is the empirical foundation for this distinction. See [[racial-stratification-mexico-city]].

## Comparative pairings

The catalog explicitly pairs each Mexico City source with its Paris counterpart so that the comparative mechanism is auditable: INEGI↔INSEE censuses, AGEB↔IRIS sub-municipal units, Casasola↔Marville photographs, Mapoteca Orozco y Berra↔David Rumsey, Gamboa↔Zola, Prieto↔Paris 1865 snapshot, Tenorio-Trillo↔Harvey on the rhetoric of universal progress.
