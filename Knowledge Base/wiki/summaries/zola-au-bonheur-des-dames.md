# Summary — Zola, *Au Bonheur des Dames* (1883)

The raw file is the Project Gutenberg French-language edition of *Au Bonheur des Dames*, the eleventh novel of the *Rougon-Macquart* cycle.

## What the novel is

Set in Paris in the late 1860s and early 1870s, the novel follows Denise Baudu, a young provincial woman who comes to Paris and finds work in a vast new department store, *Au Bonheur des Dames*, modeled on Aristide Boucicaut's Le Bon Marché. The store's expansion progressively bankrupts the small surrounding shops on the rue de la Michodière and the rue Neuve-Saint-Augustin. Owner Octave Mouret turns the store into an architectural and commercial machine for extracting the savings of bourgeois women.

## Why it matters for the project

*Au Bonheur des Dames* is the project's literary evidence for the *commercial* logic of [[haussmannization]] — the part of the Second Empire transformation that gets less attention than the demolitions and displacements but is just as important to the present-day geography:

- **The new boulevards as a retail apparatus.** The novel makes explicit that the wide Haussmannian streets, plate-glass windows, and consolidated blocks are the physical precondition for a new scale of consumer capitalism. The same boulevards Baudelaire's poor family stares at from across the road in [[baudelaire-paris-spleen]] are here described from inside, as the engine of bourgeois consumption.
- **Destruction of the small shop economy.** Zola's documentation of the small shopkeepers around the rue de la Michodière being bankrupted by the store is the literary counterpart to the displacement story told by the population data.
- **Gender and class of the new public.** Who actually walks the new boulevards and enters the new commercial spaces — bourgeois women shopping, not the working class — is part of the empirical answer to [[third-place-test]].
