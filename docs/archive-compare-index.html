Both cities show spatial concentration of planning quality. Where UQI and HDI align, inequality of life follows the map. Mexico City shows wider spread in infrastructure-linked variables; Paris shows a flatter infrastructure floor with persistent income and health gradients.

Paris compresses variation through century-old universal networks; Mexico City still encodes Porfirian and post-revolutionary unevenness in water, drainage, and transit access. The comparison is not a contest of scores but a test of whether planning grammar matches lived equality.
