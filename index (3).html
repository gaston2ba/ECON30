I · Opening

Two cities, two grammars of progress.

Walk down the Boulevard Saint-Germain in Paris and you are walking inside a decision. The wide allée, the uniform façades, the angled intersections — all of it was drawn on a map in the 1850s by Baron Haussmann and stamped onto the city by imperial decree. Walk through Iztapalapa in Mexico City and you are walking inside a different kind of decision: the absence of one. The streets bend around hillsides. Houses rose first, sewers came later, deeds came later still. Both cities are the result of urban planningUrban planningThe deliberate organization of land, infrastructure, and public space — including zoning, transit, parks, and housing. At its best a public good; at its worst a tool of social control.. One was planned by the state. The other was planned by everyone except the state.

If urban planning is one of the great engines of progress, then the two cities ask the same question in opposite voices: progress for whom?

This project asks whether we can measure the quality of urban planning at the neighborhood level, and whether that quality predicts how rich or poor a neighborhood is. We compare the 20 arrondissements of Paris with the 16 alcaldías of Mexico City — units of roughly the same scale, in cities that are, in almost every other respect, opposites.

The result, we hope, is a small contribution to a much older conversation: about who gets to draw the lines, who has to live inside them, and what spills across the boundary in either direction.

What is urban planning?

Urban planning is the deliberate organization of land, infrastructure, and public space. At its best it is a public good — sewers, parks, transit, libraries, the grid of a walkable street. At its worst it is a tool of social control — the displacement of the poor, the destruction of neighborhoods, the drawing of lines that segregate as effectively as any law.

IV · Our Index

Introducing the Urban Quality Index.

The UQI
Urban Quality Index · 0 to 100

To compare neighborhoods across two very different cities, we built an index that combines six measurable features of how well a neighborhood is built and served. Each component is, as best we can manage, upstream of income: planned features that influence economic outcomes, rather than market amenities that simply follow money.

Toggle components on or off below to see how the index reshapes itself. Click "Why?" on any component to see what it measures and why we chose it.

Build the UQI 6 of 6 active
All components are normalized to a 0–100 scale within each city before being averaged, so scores remain comparable across Paris and Mexico City despite differences in absolute levels.

* What we left out

Coffee shops, restaurants, museums, and other commercial amenities are deliberately excluded. These are downstream of income — wealthy neighborhoods attract them; poor ones do not. Including them in the index would partly explain income with itself, inflating the apparent strength of any planning–income relationship.

We instead use them as outcome variables in our analysis: where good planning is in place, do third-space amenities follow? This is a stronger empirical question than the alternative.

Museums sit in an ambiguous middle ground — historically placed by political decisions (especially in Paris), but increasingly clustering near wealth. We track them separately rather than fold them into the UQI.

VI · Findings

Does planning predict income?

Our central regression takes the form:

log( Median Incomei ) = α + β · UQIi + γ · Xi + εi

where i indexes neighborhoods, X contains controls (population, distance to center), run separately for each city.

We expect that the relationship between planning quality and income will be positive and strong in Paris, where the planned city is also the wealthy city — and weaker in Mexico City, where wealth has often grown in absence of formal planning (Polanco, Santa Fe) and where many high-quality public spaces exist in middle-income areas.

If the gap holds up, it points at something larger: the same planning amenity is worth more, in income terms, in a city where planning is the rule than in one where it is the exception.

One reading is that planning concentrates value: where the state has invested, land becomes scarce and expensive, and the wealthy capture it. Another reading is that planning is more substitutable in Mexico City: residents have built their own substitutes for parks, transit, and third spaces over decades, so the formal versions matter less. We do not yet know which is closer to true.

Voices from the era

Both cities' planning was contested at the time, not just in retrospect. A short list of texts that shaped the debates we are still having.

  • Paris · 1872 · Pamphlet Jules Ferry, Les Comptes Fantastiques d'Haussmann — the famous attack on Haussmann's bookkeeping that helped end his career. Read on Gallica
  • Paris · 1863 · Poetry Charles Baudelaire, Le Cygne — "le vieux Paris n'est plus." The poet on the city Haussmann was erasing. Read at fleursdumal.org
  • Mexico City · 1933 · Treatise Carlos Contreras, Plano Regulador del Distrito Federal — Mexico City's first comprehensive zoning plan, a Beaux-Arts vision the metropolis quickly outgrew.
  • Mexico City · 1972 · Sociology Larissa Adler Lomnitz, Cómo sobreviven los marginados — the foundational study of social networks in colonias populares. Publisher
  • Both · 1961 · Manifesto Jane Jacobs, The Death and Life of Great American Cities — the case against modernist planning that reframed how both cities are debated today.
